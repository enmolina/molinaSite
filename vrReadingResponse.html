Virtual Reality


The topic of virtual reality has always been of interest to me, as is the case with many people, given its promising potential to blur the lines between tangible and virtual reality. The Verge article draws parallels between VR’s “metaphysical transportation to an altered state” and the “escapism” offered by drugs, alcohol and sex. Such a concept never really crossed my mind, and it raises the question of whether VR technology brings with it any negative implications involving overuse or dependencies.

To date, however, I still believe VR is in its earliest stages and must be explored further before being widely adopted. The interview with Brandon Jones from Google highlights an important consideration for virtual reality; “the act of putting on and taking off a VR headset,” he says, is “an essentially destructive activity.” There is no question that VR holds potential for the gaming community, however the functionality of VR technology is still too bulky and destructive to integrate seamlessly with actual reality. This also begs the question of the validity of the term “augmented reality.” Is VR really taking reality and amplifying and improving it? With the exception of VR gaming experiences – which I have yet to experience but can imagine it being an immersive experience – VR fails to offer an experience better than the real thing.



This exploration of augmented reality is the focus of my thesis project, which is why I really enjoyed dissecting these readings and gaining insights from experienced professionals with different opinions on the matter. Personally, I am interested in the use of VR technology specifically at music festivals and concerts. As an avid concertgoer myself, I would love the opportunity to experience and “relive” the shows I’ve been to through virtual reality. I have a nostalgic soul when it comes to music, and the ability to “transport back” to my favorite concert moments would be truly special. Similarly, I would find great value in experiencing a music festival I could not attend via virtual reality. To me, that would be the second best thing.



While similar ideas are floating around today, like the real estate walkthrough example mentioned in Jones’ interview, VR still needs improvements on the functionality and integration of its hardware to offer more immersive environments to users.

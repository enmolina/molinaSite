The Web is Dead | Open Web


The two articles chosen for discussion this week focus on the idea that the Web is "dead," or is at least on a decline after about two decades of existence. The Wired Magazine article attributes this demise to the introduction of mobile devices, tablets and their relative applications. Not only do consumers prefer to access information from apps on their mobile devices, but businesses too have shifted their marketing efforts from the Web to mobile. During my summer internship at PwC, for example, I experienced firsthand the concept of "mobile first" in software development. Any time my team was required to design and sketch up wireframes for a new feature or web page, we designed the mobile wireframe first, and then used that to build out the more complex desktop design. PwC is only one of many firms designing by these standards, which focus on enhancing the user experience on the mobile web, which is evidently used more frequently than the desktop web.

Despite the rise of mobile, the web is certainly not dead. The 2010 Wired Magazine article claims that the need for the web as a digital presence and a source of information is declining, as the rise of mobile apps and tablets replace the original methods of information sharing and media consumption. Although it is valid to say that people are accessing digital information from their mobile devices and tablets more frequently than they did five years ago, it is unreasonable to assume that this new digital trend has completely "killed" the web.



Five years after this article published, the web continues to be one of the primary sources of information worldwide. Apps are indeed the preferable method of information retrieval for many consumers, who prefer when "the screen comes to them, they don't have to go to the screen." We must also consider, however, whether these mobile apps are in fact helping or hurting the web. In many cases, apps are a gateway to the web. Any link that is opened from major apps like Facebook and Twitter, for example, link directly to mobile friendly web pages. Although people are spending less time accessing the web on their desktop computers, they are accessing the web nonetheless. More time on their mobile phones just means people are changing the ways in which they access the web.



An article published in 2014 (ironically, by Wired Magazine as well) supports the idea that the rise of mobile has only allowed the web to continue its growth. The article lists numerous statistics that show an increase in web usage on both mobile and desktop; perhaps the most significant figure, however, is the fact that the amount of data on the web is expected to more than double in the next three years. Similarly, the amount of data on the mobile web is expected to increase by more than 10 times its current size. The article comes to an end by admitting these facts and figures as "a clear indication of growth," rather than signs of a "dying entity."
